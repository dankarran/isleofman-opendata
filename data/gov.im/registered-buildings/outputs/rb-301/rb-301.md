Register entry description 
History 
Finished in 1806. Built by Thomas Corrin on the highest point of his family estate, and 
intended as a mausoleum for himself. 
The tower is known to have been used as an official shipping landmark since 1840. 
Details 
MATERIALS: Constructed of stone (slate) from a nearby quarry and limestone blocks from the 
south of the island. 
PLAN: Simple square plan form, each of the upper levels diminishing in size from the level 
below. 
EXTERIOR: Generally horizontally laid local stone, with limestone blocks used on each corner. 
The building has four levels, each level stepping inboard slightly from the level below. The 

 
building has an access door on its eastern elevation. Each elevation has centrally positioned 
blocked up openings on each storey. The building’s roof parapet has a recess at the midpoint 
of each elevation. A cone shaped finial is present at the centre of the roof. 
 
INTERIOR: The interior contains four floors, an internal flight of steps, and 12 memorial 
tablets. 
 
Subsidiary Features: Adjacent to the tower on its eastern, landward, side are two obelisks and 
a burial ground which is said to contain the remains of Thomas Corrin, his wife and her unborn 
child, and another child, Christian. The obelisks date from 1840 and 1850 respectively.
